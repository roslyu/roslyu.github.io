  • Linguistic relativity & cross‑domain translation — bridging the gap between technical language (tax, law, finance) and everyday communication
    ✻ my thought process
    • Inspired by Samuel R. Delany's Babel‑17 and the Sapir‑Whorf hypothesis — how language shapes perception. In my Master's program, we were constantly asked: "What hat are you wearing?" when reviewing something. The auditor's hat, the tax accountant's hat, the client's hat, the board of directors' hat, a random stakeholder's hat, etc. the list goes on. Each hat has its own language, assumptions, and blind spots. Tax law is a dialect few outsiders speak. I learned this viscerally when I offered volunteer help to a nonprofit — the woman on the phone heard "tax background" and assumed my skills were too niche. I had to step back and say: "I can also help with basic bookkeeping and fundraising budgets. I speak that language too." Being an expert isn't just knowing the rules — it's knowing how to translate them for the room you're in.

    ✻ my thought process
    • Fascinated by how vague legal language requires constant interpretation, precedent‑seeking, and judgment. At PwC, I relied on prior treatments and collaborative research to navigate ambiguous tax rules, much like the constitutional elasticity established in Marbury v. Madison. 🌍 Culture & Community
  • Cultural shifts toward personal identity, depth, privacy, and authentic communities — and how organizations can honor these values
  • Motivation, drive & team morale — rethinking what actually motivates people in high‑pressure environments
    ✻ my thought process
    • I noticed I wasn't motivated by typical incentives — happy hours, team lunches, social events. Deep down, those felt like more work, not less. I already had an outside life and family I wanted to spend that time with. Many professionals I've worked with have felt the same way. We often discussed team morale together, and a recurring insight was this: the best way to improve morale and lower stress is to remove pressure, not add more activities. Real motivation comes from trust, flexibility, and reducing unnecessary friction — not from forced togetherness.
    • At the same time, I noticed that building genuine trust through casual conversations and open questions helped me grow close to my team naturally. I considered them all friends — not because of any planned outings, but through simple daily interactions and scheduling intentional time to get to know them. During non‑busy seasons, when there was flexibility and space, those relationships deepened organically. That flexibility and trust became the foundation of genuine motivation — the kind that made me want to show up for my team, not because I had to, but because I cared about them. And once that foundation was there, the connections came naturally — like breathing air.
    • And because that trust was already there, it made communication during high‑pressure situations significantly easier. We could move past issues faster, give each other direct feedback without fear, and focus on solving problems rather than managing egos or navigating office politics. The foundation we built in the quiet times carried us through the loud ones.
